ADA Parking Lot Requirements: Property Manager's Guide

Parking lots are a common ADA exposure point because violations are visible, measurable, and often inexpensive to allege. The goal is not panic. The goal is documentation and prioritization.

Accessible parking compliance is more than blue paint. Property teams need to look at stall count, van-accessible spaces, access aisle width, signage height and location, pavement markings, slopes, routes to entries, curb ramps, vertical changes, and whether resurfacing changes any of those conditions.

Accessible stalls and access aisles: Confirm the required quantity, dimensions, markings, and whether the access aisle is shared correctly.
Van-accessible parking: Verify van signage, access aisle width, and vertical clearance where applicable.
Slopes: Accessible stalls and aisles are especially sensitive because a small slope miss can become a technical violation.
Path of travel: The accessible route from parking to the building must avoid abrupt level changes, excessive cross slope, blocked paths, and missing curb ramps.
Documentation: Photos, measurements, repair logs, and contractor scopes matter when a complaint arrives.

Why Resurfacing Raises the Stakes

Sealcoat, overlay, concrete replacement, curb work, and restriping can all affect accessible parking conditions. A simple paving project can accidentally change grades, bury transitions, move striping, or create a noncompliant path if ADA is treated as an afterthought.

A Practical Prioritization Framework

First, correct obvious field conditions that create user barriers or visible litigation targets. Second, document measurements and contractor responsibilities. Third, build ADA scope review into every surface project before work starts. This creates a defensible process instead of a scramble after a demand letter.
