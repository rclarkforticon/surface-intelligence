Why pavement affects NOI

Pavement maintenance is an operating expense that directly reduces net operating income when spent — but deferred maintenance converts that small annual cost into a large capital emergency. Emergency spend disrupts operations, triggers tenant complaints, and compresses asset value at exactly the wrong time.

For value-add investors and long-term holds: proactive maintenance reduces total lifecycle cost by 30–50% compared to reactive repair and avoids the forced replacement events that hit NOI hardest.

▎Common questions

How does deferred pavement maintenance affect property NOI?

Deferred maintenance doesn't eliminate cost — it concentrates it. When pavement deteriorates past the point of routine maintenance, the required intervention jumps from sealcoating or crack sealing to full reconstruction. That's often a 5–8x cost increase in a single capital event, which compresses NOI significantly in the year it hits and may require emergency financing.

What cap rate impact does pavement condition have?

In a cap-rate valuation, property value equals NOI divided by cap rate. A large one-time pavement reconstruction event reduces NOI in that year, directly reducing the property's appraised value. Additionally, visible pavement deterioration can affect tenant retention, perceived asset quality, and the cap rate applied by a buyer during due diligence. This tool models both the spend and the value implications.

When does pavement maintenance become a capital event?

Generally when the scope exceeds routine maintenance (sealcoating, crack sealing, minor patching) and moves into structural rehabilitation or full reconstruction. This typically happens when PCI drops below 40–50, when base failure is present, or when drainage deficiencies require grade correction. At that point, the cost often exceeds $2–4/sq ft and may require board approval, financing, or reserve drawdown.
