▎Common questions

How much notice do tenants need for paving work?

For commercial tenants, check your lease for specific notice requirements — many commercial leases specify 24–72 hours minimum for work that affects parking or access. For residential tenants, California law generally requires at least 24 hours notice for non-emergency access, though 7–14 days is best practice for parking lot work that displaces vehicles. Phased work with clear maps typically generates far fewer complaints than short-notice, full-lot closures.

What should a paving work notice include?

Project start and end dates, affected parking areas (with a map if possible), work hours, alternative parking location if available, contractor contact for day-of questions, and property management contact for concerns. For phased work, include the phase sequence and when each area reopens. The generator handles all of this automatically.

Is tenant notification required for parking lot paving?

Legally, it depends on your lease terms and local ordinances, but practically it's always required if you want to avoid towing disputes, tenant complaints, and property management liability. Documented notification history also protects you if a tenant claims they weren't informed. This tool generates that documentation automatically as part of the letter output.
